PAPER NAME:
    Graph Retrieval-Augmented Generation: A Survey

AUTHORS:
    Boci Peng¹, Yun Zhu², Yongchao Liu³, Xiaohe Bo⁴, Haizhou Shi⁵, 
    Chuntao Hong³, Yan Zhang¹*, Siliang Tang²
    
    ¹ School of Intelligence Science and Technology, Peking University, China
    ² College of Computer Science and Technology, Zhejiang University, China
    ³ Ant Group, China
    ⁴ Gaoling School of Artificial Intelligence, Renmin University of China
    ⁵ Rutgers University, US
    
    * Corresponding Author
    Published: September 2024 | arXiv:2408.08921

ABSTRACT:
    Recently, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has achieved remarkable 
    success in addressing the challenges of Large Language Models (LLMs) 
    without necessitating retraining. By referencing an external knowledge 
    base, RAG refines LLM outputs, effectively mitigating issues such as 
    "hallucination", lack of domain-specific knowledge, and outdated 
    information. However, the complex structure of relationships among 
    different entities in databases presents challenges for RAG systems. 
    In response, GraphRAG leverages structural information across entities 
    to enable more precise and comprehensive retrieval, capturing relational 
    knowledge and facilitating more accurate, context-aware responses. Given 
    the novelty and potential of GraphRAG, a systematic review of current 
    technologies is imperative. This paper provides the first comprehensive 
    overview of GraphRAG methodologies. We formalize the GraphRAG workflow, 
    encompassing Graph-Based Indexing, Graph-Guided Retrieval, and 
    Graph-Enhanced Generation.

METHODOLOGY:
    The paper formalizes GraphRAG into three stages:
    
    1. G-INDEXING (Graph-Based Indexing):
       - Construct/identify graph database aligned with downstream tasks
       - Graph data sources: Open Knowledge Graphs (Wikidata, Freebase, 
         DBpedia, YAGO, ConceptNet) or Self-Constructed Graphs
       - Indexing methods: Graph Indexing, Text Indexing, Vector Indexing, 
         Hybrid Indexing
       
    2. G-RETRIEVAL (Graph-Guided Retrieval):
       - Extract pertinent information from graph database given user query
       - Retrieval granularities: Nodes, Triplets, Paths, Subgraphs, Hybrid
       - Retrievers: Non-parametric, LM-based, GNN-based
       - Retrieval paradigms: Once, Iterative (adaptive/non-adaptive), 
         Multi-stage
       - Enhancement techniques: Query expansion, Query decomposition, 
         Knowledge merging, Knowledge pruning
       
    3. G-GENERATION (Graph-Enhanced Generation):
       - Synthesize meaningful outputs based on retrieved graph data
       - Generators: GNNs, Language Models, Hybrid Models
       - Graph formats for LMs: Adjacency tables, Natural language, 
         Code-like forms, Syntax trees, Node sequences, Graph embeddings
       - Enhancement: Pre-generation, Mid-generation, Post-generation
    
    Mathematical Formulation:
       y* = argmax P(y|q, G)
       
       Where y* is optimal answer given query q and graph G
       
       Decomposed as:
       P(y|q, G) ≈ P_θ(y|q, G*) × P_φ(G*|q, G)
       
       Where G* is optimal subgraph, θ = generator params, φ = retriever params

FINDINGS:
    1. LIMITATIONS OF TRADITIONAL RAG:
       - Neglecting Relationships: Traditional RAG fails to capture 
         structured relational knowledge beyond semantic similarity
       - Redundant Information: Text concatenation leads to "lost in the 
         middle" problem with excessively long contexts
       - Lacking Global Information: RAG retrieves only document subsets, 
         missing comprehensive global context
    
    2. GRAPHRAG ADVANTAGES:
       - Captures relational knowledge through graph elements (nodes, 
         triplets, paths, subgraphs)
       - Knowledge graphs offer abstraction/summarization, shortening 
         input length
       - Graph communities enable comprehensive information access for 
         Query-Focused Summarization
    
    3. KEY FINDINGS ON RETRIEVERS:
       - Non-parametric retrievers: High efficiency but may suffer from 
         inaccurate retrieval without task-specific training
       - LM-based retrievers: Higher accuracy but significant computational 
         overhead
       - GNN-based retrievers: Adept at leveraging complex graph structures
       - Hybrid approaches recommended for balancing efficiency and accuracy
    
    4. RETRIEVAL GRANULARITY INSIGHTS:
       - Nodes: Precise but limited contextual breadth
       - Triplets: Structured but may lack indirect relationships
       - Paths: Captures reasoning chains but exponential complexity
       - Subgraphs: Comprehensive but computationally expensive
       - Hybrid granularities often best for complex scenarios
    
    5. FUTURE RESEARCH DIRECTIONS:
       - Dynamic and Adaptive Graphs (real-time updates)
       - Multi-Modality Information Integration (images, audio, video)
       - Scalable and Efficient Retrieval for billion-entity graphs
       - Integration with Graph Foundation Models
       - Lossless Compression of Retrieved Context
       - Standard Benchmarks for evaluation
       - Broader Applications (healthcare, finance, legal, smart cities)

PAPER NAME:
    From Local to Global: A Graph RAG Approach to Query-Focused Summarization

AUTHORS:
    Darren Edge, Ha Trinh, Newman Cheng, Joshua Bradley, Alex Chao, 
    Apurva Mody, Steven Truitt, Jonathan Larson
    
    Microsoft Research
    Published: April 2024 | arXiv:2404.16130

ABSTRACT:
    The use of retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) to retrieve relevant 
    information from an external knowledge source enables large language 
    models (LLMs) to answer questions over private and/or previously unseen 
    document collections. However, RAG fails on global questions directed 
    at an entire text corpus, such as "What are the main themes in the 
    dataset?", since this is inherently a query-focused summarization (QFS) 
    task, rather than an explicit retrieval task. Prior methods addressing 
    QFS do not scale to the volumes of text indexed by typical RAG systems. 
    In response, we propose a Graph RAG approach to question answering over 
    private text corpora that scales with both the generality of user 
    questions and the quantity of source text to be indexed. Our approach 
    uses an LLM to build a graph-based text index in two stages: first to 
    derive an entity knowledge graph from the source documents, then to 
    pre-generate community summaries for all groups of closely-related 
    entities. Given a question, each community summary is used to generate 
    a partial response, from which a final response is generated in the end.

METHODOLOGY:
    TWO-STAGE INDEXING PROCESS:
    
    Stage 1 - Entity Knowledge Graph Construction:
       a) Source Document Chunking: Split documents into manageable chunks
       b) Entity & Relationship Extraction: Use LLM to extract entities 
          and relationships from each chunk
       c) Entity Resolution: Merge duplicate entities across chunks
       d) Graph Construction: Build knowledge graph from extracted 
          entities and relationships
    
    Stage 2 - Community Detection & Summarization:
       a) Apply Leiden algorithm for hierarchical community detection
       b) Detect communities at multiple resolution levels
       c) For each community: Generate LLM-based summary describing 
          the entities and their relationships
       d) Store summaries at multiple hierarchy levels
    
    QUERY-TIME PROCESSING:
       1. Given user query, retrieve relevant community summaries
       2. Each summary generates a "partial response" to the query
       3. LLM aggregates partial responses into final answer
       4. For global queries: use higher-level (coarser) summaries
       5. For local queries: use lower-level (finer) summaries
    
    MAP-REDUCE SUMMARIZATION:
       - Map Phase: Generate partial answers from each community summary
       - Reduce Phase: Aggregate partial answers into final response
       - Enables parallelization and handles large-scale corpora
    
    HIERARCHICAL COMMUNITY STRUCTURE:
       Level 0: Individual entities (finest granularity)
       Level 1: Small communities of closely related entities
       Level 2: Larger communities combining Level 1 groups
       Level N: Global/corpus-level themes (coarsest granularity)

FINDINGS:
    1. OUTPERFORMS NAIVE RAG ON GLOBAL QUERIES:
       - Traditional RAG retrieves specific passages but cannot synthesize 
         corpus-wide themes
       - Graph RAG's community summaries capture global patterns
       - Significant improvement on Query-Focused Summarization tasks
    
    2. COMPREHENSIVENESS VS. DIVERSITY TRADE-OFF:
       - Higher-level summaries: More comprehensive but less detailed
       - Lower-level summaries: More specific but may miss connections
       - Users can select appropriate level based on query type
    
    3. SCALABILITY FINDINGS:
       - Pre-computed community summaries enable efficient query-time 
         processing
       - Map-reduce approach handles large document collections
       - Trade-off: Higher indexing cost, lower query-time cost
    
    4. ENTITY RESOLUTION IMPORTANCE:
       - Proper entity resolution critical for graph quality
       - Duplicate entities fragment knowledge across communities
       - LLM-based entity resolution improves graph coherence
    
    5. COMMUNITY SIZE IMPACT:
       - Too small: Fragmented context, missing relationships
       - Too large: Diluted focus, irrelevant information
       - Leiden algorithm with resolution parameter balances this
    
    6. LLM SUMMARY QUALITY:
       - Quality of community summaries directly impacts final answers
       - Prompt engineering for summarization is crucial
       - Summaries should capture both entities AND relationships

  ✓ G-Retrieval: Single Cypher query retrieves relevant brand DNA
  ✓ G-Generation: LLM uses graph data to generate brand-aligned content

  ✓ Hybrid Approach: Combine graph structure with LLM reasoning

  ✓ Entity Resolution: Products, colors, styles are resolved entities
  ✓ Pre-computed Knowledge: Brand DNA is pre-indexed, not computed at 
    query time
  ✓ Local + Global Queries: "What's the brand color?" (local) vs 
    "What defines this brand?" (global)
